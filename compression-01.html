Lower Limb Compression
Algorithm

Australian clinical guide for compression selection, product matching, Tubigrip sizing, and patient safety monitoring.

Compression dosage hierarchy — Australia

Class 1 · Mild
18–21 mmHg
Tired/aching legs, mild oedema, DVT prophylaxis for travel, early varicose veins. Double-layer Tubigrip (~15–20 mmHg).
Class 2 · Moderate
23–32 mmHg
Moderate oedema, post-sclerotherapy, healed venous ulcers (maintenance), active venous leg ulcers (clinical target).
Class 3 · Strong
34–46 mmHg
Active venous leg ulcers, severe chronic venous insufficiency, lymphoedema. Standard 4-layer targets 40 mmHg.
Class 4 · Very Strong
>49 mmHg
Severe lymphoedema, elephantiasis, "de-bulking" of large limbs. Often requires custom fitting (short-stretch/flat-knit).

!
Graduation rule
Compression must always be graduated — highest at the ankle, decreasing up the limb — to effectively counteract venous hypertension. Never apply equal or reverse-graduated compression.

Clinical decision pathway

1
Measure ABPI
Obtain a validated Doppler ABPI before initiating any compression. Do not rely on palpation alone for high-compression (Class 2+) therapy.
2
Assess wound and oedema
Classify wound type (venous, arterial, mixed, lymphoedema). Document oedema grade, wound dimensions, and exudate level to determine required compression level.
3
Select compression class
Match the clinical picture to the appropriate compression class (see Compression Grades tab). ABPI ≥ 0.8 with no contraindications → standard compression. ABPI 0.8–0.9 → consider reduced compression (Class 1). ABPI < 0.8 → medical review before compression.
4
Choose product
Select bandage system or hosiery matching the required pressure class. Use inelastic (short-stretch) for ambulatory patients with lymphoedema; elastic (long-stretch) or multi-layer for venous ulcers.
5
Review & reassess
Reassess ABPI and wound status at minimum every 12 weeks, or sooner if clinical deterioration is noted. Adjust compression class accordingly.

Elastic vs inelastic — when to choose

Elastic (long-stretch)
High resting pressure. Suited for ambulatory and non-ambulatory patients. Most venous leg ulcer systems. Examples: Profore, Coban 2, K-Two.
Inelastic (short-stretch)
High working pressure, low resting pressure. Ideal for ambulatory lymphoedema patients. Massaging effect with calf pump. Examples: Comprilan, Putterbinde, Rosidal K.
Australian clinical standard: For active venous leg ulcers, the primary target is RAL Class 2 or 3 (23–46 mmHg). Multi-layer compression systems that achieve sustained pressure are preferred over single-layer hosiery.

Double-layer application guide

1
Measure and cut
Measure from the base of the toes to just below the knee. Cut a length of Tubigrip that is double this distance plus 2–3 cm for overlap.
2
Apply first layer
Slide the first half of the bandage onto the limb, starting at the toes and working up to just below the knee.
3
Fold back for second layer
Fold the remaining length back over the first layer. Ensure the outer layer ends 2–3 cm higher than the inner layer to prevent a pressure "ring".

Clinical caveats

The "in-between" rule
If a measurement falls exactly on the border (e.g. exactly 24 cm), choose the larger size (Size E) to avoid excessive pressure — especially if ABPI is borderline (0.8–0.9).
Joint flexion
When applying over the knee, ensure the patient has their leg slightly bent during measurement and application to prevent bunching in the popliteal fossa.
⚠ Latex warning
Standard Tubigrip contains natural rubber latex. Patients with a latex allergy must use a latex-free alternative (Tubigrip LF or Medigrip LF).

Compression classification standards comparison

In Australia, the RAL GZ 387 (German) standard is most frequently used for medical-grade therapy. The British (BS 6612) standard is common in retail and for patients requiring gentler fabric. The French (AFNOR) standard appears in some oncology settings.

When to choose each standard

Patient situation Recommended standard Reason
Active venous leg ulcer RAL Class 2 or 3 High stiffness and guaranteed high pressure required to heal the wound
Mild varicose veins / pregnancy BS Class 1 Higher compliance; easier to don; sufficient for simple prophylaxis
Fragile skin / elderly British Standard More "give" in fabric makes it easier to don without skin tears
Severe lymphoedema RAL (flat-knit) Stiffness of the German standard prevents fluid crossover into tissue

Stop & check — red flag signs

If any of the following occur, the patient must remove compression immediately and contact their clinician or present to Urgent Care.

👁
Changes in colour
Toes or fingers look pale, white, blue, or purple.
🌡
Temperature change
Foot or toes feel unusually cold compared to the other leg.
⚡
Pins & needles
Tingling, numbness, or a crawling sensation in the feet or toes.
!
Intense pain
Sharp, throbbing, or burning pain not present before compression was applied.
💧
New swelling
Toes look swollen or puffy above or below the edge of the bandage.
✋
Loss of movement
Difficulty or inability to wiggle the toes.

Capillary refill test (patient self-check)

Patients can perform this simple check to verify adequate circulation while compression is in place.

1
Press firmly on the big toenail for 5 seconds until it turns white.
2
Release the pressure completely.
✓
Pass: Pink colour returns in under 3 seconds — circulation is adequate.
✗
Fail: Stays white or takes >3 seconds — compression may be too tight. Remove and contact clinician.

Daily care do's and don'ts

Do
Check your skin at bandage edges for redness or "cutting in".
Elevate legs above your heart when sitting to help compression work.
Keep the bandage dry — use a waterproof cover when showering.
Stay active. Gentle walking helps the calf pump move blood.
Don't
Fold or roll down the top of the bandage — this creates a double-pressure ring.
Wear compression while sleeping unless your clinician specifically advised it.
Patch a hole or cut the bandage yourself if it feels tight.
Ignore a heavy or throbbing feeling that gets worse over time.
